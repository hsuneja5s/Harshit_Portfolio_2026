Shell — Finance & Deal Management draft

The entire deal lifecycle — origination to close — collapsed onto a single AI-powered platform with real-time dashboards and conversational interfaces.

TL;DR

  • Replaced spreadsheet-and-email deal management with a conversational AI surface — reducing approval cycle time materially.
  • Designed a "natural-language first, structured-form second" interaction model that worked for both finance pros and field deal-makers.
  • Shipped real-time dashboards that surface deal risk before humans need to ask.

The constraint

Shell's deal lifecycle was historically run on Excel, email, and a fragmented set of legacy tools. Approval threads spanned weeks. Risk only surfaced in steering committees — too late to act on. The mandate: collapse the cycle, surface signals earlier, without forcing finance teams to relearn their craft.

The hard part: deal-makers and finance controllers and regional analysts use the platform daily, but they want completely different things from it. A field deal-maker wants speed and natural language. A controller wants structured audit trails. Designing one surface for both was the core tension.

The decision

The biggest call: making the conversational interface the primary surface, not a chatbot bolt-on. Most enterprise AI products treat conversation as a sidebar. We made it the home screen.

Chose
Conversational primary nav. "Show me deals at risk in APAC this quarter" → live dashboard generated.
Chose
Every AI response is editable as a structured form. Deal-makers move fast in chat; controllers refine in form.
Killed
A "novice mode" with hand-holding tooltips. Patronising for a senior finance audience. Replaced with example prompts at the top.
Chose
Real-time risk dashboards built from deal data — not waiting for users to ask. Risk finds you, not the other way around.
Killed
Mobile parity at launch. Deal work happens at desks; we shipped desktop-first and saved mobile for v2.

The system

The interface is built on a "conversation primitives" design system — message blocks, structured-form blocks, dashboard blocks, and decision blocks — that interleave inside a single thread. Engineers ship new deal types by composing these primitives, not by building new screens.

AI in the loop

This was my first project where AI wasn't a feature — it was the medium. Concretely:

  • Anthropic for the conversational layer: all natural-language deal queries route through Claude with a structured tool-use schema specific to Shell's deal data. We collaborated with the engineering team on the system prompt — the design constraint was making Claude refuse confidently when data was missing rather than hallucinate.
  • Prompt-flow design with my own plugin: I used the Conversation Flow Generator (the Figma plugin I built) to draft and review hundreds of representative deal prompts before engineering wrote a line of integration code.
  • Cursor + Claude Code in prototyping: the conversational UI's interaction edges (streaming, error recovery, tool-call latency) were built as live code prototypes in Cursor. Static Figma frames couldn't capture what was happening in time.

Boundary line: AI generates insights and drafts. AI does not approve or commit deals. Every binding action requires explicit human action — a tradition I won't violate in regulated workflows.

Trade-offs

Going conversation-primary alienated a small but vocal group of senior controllers who wanted the dense table view they'd used for 15 years. We added a "classic" toggle as the escape hatch. Adoption data later showed [N]% never used it — but it was the right safety valve.

Cycle-time gains came partly from automation that shifted work from finance teams to AI-assisted draft review. Some controllers initially felt deskilled. Worth flagging — this is the underdiscussed cost of AI-native design in enterprise.

What I'd change

I'd have spent more time co-designing with controllers before the conversational shift, not after. We won the argument with leadership, then had to win it again with the people doing the work. Knowing what I know now, I'd run a "controller's roundtable" earlier — let them shape what the structured-form mode looked like, so it felt theirs.

Also: I underestimated how much error UX matters in conversational AI products. Users are far less forgiving of an AI mistake than a form validation error. Investing in error states earlier would've prevented a small adoption stutter at launch.
