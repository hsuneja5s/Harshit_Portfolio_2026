Insight.ai

The AI layer that surfaces deal risks, budget anomalies, and spend trajectories for a Fortune 10 energy company — before finance leaders know to ask. Built into Pacific Platform, operating across 70+ countries.

RoleSenior Product Designer
ClientGlobal Energy Major (Fortune 10, Operating in 70+ Countries)
TimelineJan 2025 — Jun 2025

In brief

  • The problem wasn't reporting — it was time-to-decision. Finance leaders at a Fortune 10 energy company were learning about deal-level risk weeks after it became actionable.
  • The bet: embed AI inside the workflow finance leaders already lived in, not behind a separate analytics tool. Insight.ai sits where budget decisions actually get made.
  • The shift: finance moved from monthly retrospective reporting to in-flight risk management — collapsing the gap between signal and decision.

The user problem

Finance leaders running deal portfolios at a Fortune 10 energy company weren't short on data — they were short on time-to-decision. Deal data, time bookings, and budget actuals lived in four parallel trackers. By the time a deal showed up as "at risk" in a monthly steering committee deck, the window to redirect resourcing or renegotiate scope had already closed.

This wasn't a dashboard problem. A better dashboard reports the past faster. The actual cost was strategic: capital-allocation calls that should have been adjusted in week 2 of a quarter were getting flagged in week 10. The business cost showed up downstream as overrun budgets, deferred recoveries, and the slow erosion of margin on flagship engagements.

A dashboard reports the past. A cockpit lets the pilot fly.

Treating this as a feature problem — "build a better risk view" — would have shipped a prettier version of the same monthly cycle. The reframe was that this was a cycle-time problem, and AI was the only realistic way to compress it without doubling the size of the finance ops team.

The bet

We made one explicit call early: Insight.ai had to live inside the day-to-day deal workflow, not next to it. A standalone analytics product would have shipped faster and demoed better. It also would have failed adoption — finance leaders aren't going to add a fifth tool to a workflow they're already trying to consolidate.

What we chose not to optimize for: surface area. We deliberately sacrificed forecasting modules, scenario analysis, custom-report builders, and configurable dashboards. Every one of those was on a wishlist. Every one was deferred because adoption depended on the cockpit being legible to a finance lead opening it cold — not on the platform demonstrating its full capability surface.

Partner-level call: we'd rather ship a tool people use weekly than a tool that wins the procurement RFP and sits unused.

How we built it

Four views — same navigation, same vocabulary, same data spine — each answering a question finance leaders ask weekly, not quarterly.

Deal Management
Every active deal as a structured record. Portfolio at a glance, drillable to a single deal.
Financial Dashboard
Portfolio KPIs with one-click drill. The "is the spend paying off?" view.
Resources & Time
Who's on which deal, booked time, capacity hot spots.
Budget Utilization
Planned vs. actual spend. Where Insight.ai lives.

Navigation is exception-first. At risk, over budget, capacity constrained are primary chips, not buried filters. The design call: leaders don't open the platform to see the portfolio — they open it to find the few deals that need them this week. Two taps from cold-open to action.

Insight.ai

Insight.ai is the AI layer embedded inside the Budget view. It answers — proactively, with citation — the question finance teams ask every week: "is this deal off track, and how much warning do I have?"

  • Burn-rate alerts: "Burn rate up 18% this month. Budget exhaustion projected 3 weeks before closure." Surfaces during the quarter, not after.
  • Cost-by-category anomalies: flags categories where actuals diverge sharply from plan, with comparison window cited inline.
  • Spend-trend forward views: projects FY trajectories from current burn, citing the historical comparison the projection is anchored on.

Every Insight.ai output has a "why this" affordance — the numbers, the comparison window, the calculation. In a regulated finance context, trust comes from verification, not faith. That call is why the feature passed internal risk review on the first pass and didn't end up gated behind a manual approval queue.

Business outcome

The design decisions tied directly to three business outcomes the CFO function cared about:

↓ Time-to-decision
Deal-level risk surfaced in-flight, not in monthly steering. Capital-allocation calls happen earlier in the quarter — when adjustments still change the outcome.
↑ Margin protection
Budget anomalies flagged before they compound. Insight.ai's burn-rate signals catch overruns weeks earlier than slide-pack reporting did.
↓ License costs
Retiring four legacy trackers and the spreadsheet infrastructure around them drove measurable cost reduction — directly attributable to the consolidation decision.

What we didn't ship

Three things were on the wishlist and stayed there. Worth being honest about:

  • Forecasting and scenario analysis. The natural next layer once burn-rate data is structured. Deferred because adoption of the core cockpit had to come first — a forecasting tool finance leaders don't trust the underlying data of is worse than no forecasting tool. Earned, not assumed.
  • Custom report builders for business-unit finance leads. Several BUs wanted their own dashboards. We held the line on a single shared vocabulary across the portfolio, knowing it would cost us configurability. The right call for v1 — but it's a debt the v2 governance model needs to pay down.
  • A first-class contributor experience for deal owners. The cockpit was designed leader-out: it serves the finance lead opening it weekly. Deal owners and analysts — the people keeping the data fresh — got a thinner experience. Their flows lagged leadership flows by a release cycle. If I were starting again, I'd parallel-track contributors from day one.
