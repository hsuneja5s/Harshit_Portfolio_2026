Central Union Bank — Digital Banking draft

TL;DR

  • Lifted NPS from 4.7 → 9.2 in a single release cycle by reframing the app around conversational primary navigation.
  • Shifted 30%+ of customers from branch / call-centre to self-service for the highest-volume tasks.
  • Designed doorstep banking flows — from request to officer arrival — for a customer base that genuinely doesn't want to come to the branch.

The constraint

Central Union Bank's customer base skews older and trust-sensitive. The previous app was the worst kind of "modernised" — a screen-by-screen port of branch processes, full of regulatory disclaimers and dropdowns that no one used. NPS at 4.7 reflected what customers told us in interviews: "the bank doesn't trust me to know what I want."

The constraint we couldn't move: RBI compliance for every flow. Every consent screen, every disclaimer, every two-factor confirmation — locked. We had to redesign experience around an immovable regulatory shell.

The decision

The strategic call: stop modelling the app on branch processes. Model it on how customers describe what they want. They don't say "I want to perform a third-party fund transfer." They say "I want to pay my son's college fees." Conversational primary nav was the answer.

Chose
Conversational home screen. "What do you want to do today?" with intelligent autocomplete trained on common phrasings.
Chose

The system

The redesign was anchored on a "task-first" component library — every task type (transfer, pay bill, deposit, investment, doorstep request, dispute) gets one canonical card composition. Engineers ship new tasks by adding to the registry, not by designing new screens.

AI in the loop

AI's role here was deliberately quiet. In retail banking, customers don't want AI showing off — they want the bank to get out of their way.

  • Conversational autocomplete: the home screen's natural-language input was powered by an intent-classifier that mapped phrases like "send money to my mom" or "fees ka payment" to the right task. We trained it on real customer service transcripts (with consent, anonymised).
  • Internal copy-review loop: all customer-facing microcopy passed through Claude as a tone-and-clarity check before going to legal. Caught hundreds of legalese phrasings that legal then signed off on simpler versions of.
  • I did not use AI for: personalised recommendations, investment advisory, or anything that touched fiduciary territory. That line is non-negotiable in retail banking.

Trade-offs

The conversational home is slower for power users who already memorised the legacy app's tab structure. We absorbed that cost — these users were ~5% of the base, and the change was net-positive within two release cycles even for them.

Killing gamified goals lost us a small contingent of younger customers. But the goal was a unified experience, not a segmented one — and the older majority's NPS lift more than paid for it.

What I'd change

I'd have invested in onboarding for the legacy power user. Our launch focused on making the new experience welcoming to first-time and casual users. We didn't realise until week two that the most-vocal complaints were from heavy users who couldn't find the tab structure they'd internalised. A 30-second "what changed" tour at launch would have saved a lot of support load.

And: I'd push harder on accessibility audits earlier. We caught a few WCAG issues at the eleventh hour that should have been part of the design system spec from week one.
