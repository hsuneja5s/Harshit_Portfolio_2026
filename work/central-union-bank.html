Central Union Bank — CUB.ai

Designing an AI banking assistant that could actually handle everyday banking. Resolves routine queries and guides customers through common tasks — reducing call-center load and making self-service feel closer to a human conversation.

TL;DR

  • Designed CUB.ai — an AI assistant inside the bank's app — that resolves the most common customer service requests without sending them to a call center.
  • Built on a quick-action chip model: Banking Summary, Last 5 Transactions, Block Card, Transfer Money, Pay Bills, Find ATM, Download Statement, Report Fraud — each backed by a specific intent + structured response.
  • Lifted NPS from 4.7 → 9.2 in a single release cycle and shifted 30%+ of customer interactions away from branch + call center.

The constraint

Central Union Bank's customer base skews older and trust-sensitive. The previous app was the worst kind of "modernised" — a screen-by-screen port of branch processes, full of regulatory disclaimers and dropdowns no one used. NPS sat at 4.7. In interviews, customers told us: "the bank doesn't trust me to know what I want."

The constraint we couldn't move: RBI compliance for every flow. Every consent screen, every disclaimer, every two-factor confirmation — locked. We had to redesign experience around an immovable regulatory shell, while making the result feel less like a form gauntlet and more like a conversation.

Layered on top: an older audience who say "I want to pay my son's college fees", not "I want to perform a third-party fund transfer." The product needed to meet customers in their language — not the bank's.

The decision

The strategic call: stop modelling the app on branch processes. Model it on how customers describe what they want. Conversational primary surface (CUB.ai) backed by quick-action chips — for the long tail, fall through to structured forms. For the head — the 8 most-used tasks — chat handles end-to-end.

Chose
A chat-first AI assistant with quick-action chips for the top 8 tasks: Banking Summary, Last 5 Transactions, Block Card, Transfer Money, Pay Bills, Find ATM, Download Statement, Report Fraud.
Chose
Structured response cards inside the chat — when a customer asks for "Last 5 Transactions", they get a tabular response with merchant, amount, date, category. Not a paragraph.
Chose
A unified design system underneath. Account Management screens, the chatbot surface, and shared components share one token tree, one type scale, one accessibility baseline.
Killed
Free-form open chat as the entry point. Tested with target users — they froze at the empty input field. Quick-action chips solved the cold-start problem and let conversation be the medium, not the prerequisite.
Killed
Gamified savings goals + animated mascots. Tested with the older audience — felt patronising. Customers wanted respect, not stickers.

The system

CUB.ai is composed from four conversational primitives: greeting messages, quick-action chips, structured-response cards (e.g., transaction tables, account summaries), and confirmation/handoff blocks (for anything that needs a 2FA or a branch escalation).

When a customer taps "Last 5 Transactions", the response renders as a structured card inside the chat — merchant name, amount, date, category — not a wall of natural-language text. Same for "Banking Summary" (account balances + recent activity) and "Find ATM" (map embed with nearest 3 results).

The design system that powers CUB.ai also powers Account Management screens, login/onboarding, and the broader app. Same tokens, same components — so the chatbot surface feels native to the bank, not bolted on.

AI in the loop

AI's role here was deliberately quiet. In retail banking — and especially with an older demographic — customers don't want AI showing off. They want the bank to get out of their way.

  • Intent classification: the chip selections + free-text inputs feed into an intent classifier that maps phrasings ("send money to my mom", "fees ka payment") to one of the supported tasks. We trained it on real (consented, anonymised) customer-service transcripts.
  • Tone-and-clarity review: all customer-facing microcopy passed through Claude as a tone-and-clarity check before going to legal. Caught hundreds of legalese phrasings that legal then signed off on simpler versions of.
  • Conversation flow drafting: I used the Conversation Flow Generator Figma plugin (which I built) to draft and review hundreds of representative customer prompts before engineering wrote integration code.

Where AI doesn't go: personalised investment recommendations, advisory, anything that touches fiduciary territory. Off-limits. A retail bank cannot have an AI nudging customers toward financial products — full stop.

Trade-offs

The chip-first approach is slower for power users who already memorised the legacy app's tab structure. We absorbed that cost — those users were ~5% of the base, and the change was net-positive within two release cycles even for them.

Killing gamified goals lost us a small contingent of younger customers. But the goal was a unified experience, not a segmented one — and the older majority's NPS lift more than paid for it.

Constraining to 8 quick actions left ~15% of customer-service requests still falling through to call center. We accepted it for v1 — the head of the curve was the leverage point. The long tail is v2.

What I'd change

I'd have invested in onboarding for the legacy power user. Our launch focused on making the new experience welcoming to first-time and casual users. We didn't realise until week two that the most-vocal complaints were from heavy users who couldn't find the tab structure they'd internalised. A 30-second "what changed" tour at launch would have saved a lot of support load.

And: I'd push harder on error UX in the chatbot earlier. Users are far less forgiving of an AI mistake than a form validation error. A misclassified intent feels personal in a way a dropdown error doesn't. Investing in those edges from day one would have prevented a small adoption stutter at launch.
