TL;DR

  • Designed and shipped India's first accessibility-first diagnostic app for Torrent — book tests, upload prescriptions, track health trends in one place.
  • Reframed the product from "download a PDF report" to "a daily health companion" — Records section turns one-off lab values into HbA1c / Cholesterol / LDL trends over time.
  • Reports delivered in under 6 hours. Senior-citizen flat 10% off built into the home screen. Chat & Book Test as primary CTA.

The constraint

Most diagnostic apps in India treat lab reports as paperwork. You book a test, you wait, you get a PDF. Then the report sits in an email until your next doctor visit — by which point you've forgotten what your last HbA1c reading was.

For Torrent's patient base — heavily senior + chronic-condition (diabetes, cardiac, thyroid) — that workflow doesn't match how they actually live with their condition. They're tracking the same handful of values for years. The product needed to surface trend, not transaction.

Hard constraint: the audience skews older and lower digital literacy. Every interaction had to clear an accessibility bar — large tap targets, voice-first paths, regional language support, high-contrast text — without looking like a "senior app" that younger users wouldn't open.

The decision

The framing call: stop treating this as a "booking app with reports attached." Reframe as a health companion with three jobs — make tests easy to book, make reports easy to understand, make trends impossible to ignore.

Chose
A Records-first home navigation with three tabs: Reports, Prescription, Trends. Trends gets equal weight with Reports because trends are why patients keep coming back.
Chose
Chat & Book Test as the primary CTA — not a search-first booking flow. Older patients freeze at empty search inputs.
Chose
Senior-citizen flat 10% offer surfaced as a banner on home, not buried in a Promotions tab. The audience is the audience — design accordingly.
Chose
Trend visualizations show a value's position relative to the ideal range, not just a number. "Your recent HbA1c is rising and nearing the high end of the ideal range" is the headline; the chart is supporting evidence.
Killed
Gamified streaks for "consistent testing." Tested with target users — felt insulting to people with serious conditions. Replaced with quiet trend signals.
Killed
A subscription-style "premium" tier in v1. We needed adoption first; monetisation gate-keeping at launch would have killed the audience we needed to validate the product with.

The system

The app is composed from a small set of health-aware components — search-by-location bar, package cards (with sample type + fasting requirement), test detail blocks, trend cards (with ideal range overlays), and a unified Records view. Every component carries the same accessibility baseline: 44pt minimum tap target, AA contrast minimum, voice-readable.

AI in the loop

AI's role here is quiet and clinical — not a feature, an interpretive layer.

  • Plain-language trend interpretation: the natural-language summary on each trend card ("Your recent HbA1c is rising and nearing the high end of the ideal range") is generated from the patient's own historical values. The phrasing is reviewed by a clinical advisory before going live in any of 5 supported categories.
  • Prescription OCR: the Upload Prescription flow uses on-device OCR to extract suggested tests from a photographed paper prescription. Patients confirm before booking — the AI suggests, the patient decides.
  • Conversation flow design: the Chat & Book Test conversational entry was prototyped using my Conversation Flow Generator Figma plugin — drafted hundreds of patient phrasings ("I want a diabetes test," "blood test for fees check-up," "recurring thyroid test") to train the intent layer.

What AI absolutely doesn't do: provide medical advice, suggest treatment, interpret reports beyond statistical context. That line sits between "your value is in/out of normal range" and "you should do X about it." The first is OK; the second is not the app's role.

Trade-offs

Choosing a senior-first audience meant the visual language is calmer, simpler, and slightly less "modern app" than a younger-skewing diagnostic app would be. We absorbed the trade-off — younger users adapt to calmer interfaces; senior users genuinely struggle with the visual density of most consumer apps.

The trends-first focus deferred a few revenue-adjacent features: doctor referral, e-pharmacy integration, family member reports. All are on the roadmap; none made v1. Adoption first, monetisation second.

Voice-first booking is slower than typed search for power users. The path forward is layered — voice + chip selection on top of optional advanced search — rather than splitting the audience.

The product is in the hands of patients now. The deeper measure of success is whether patients keep coming back to look at their trends — which is a 6-month signal we'll be watching carefully.

What I'd change

I'd have invested earlier in family member workflows. A surprising amount of trend-checking is done by adult children on behalf of parents. We deferred it to v2; if I'd front-loaded it, the early-adoption curve would likely have been steeper.

And: I'd have shipped doctor-shareable summary cards from day one. Patients want to bring their phone to a doctor visit and show "here's what's been changing." We have it on the roadmap; should have launched with it.
