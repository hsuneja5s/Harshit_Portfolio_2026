TL;DR

  • Designed and shipped India's first accessibility-first diagnostic app for Torrent — book tests, upload prescriptions, track health trends in one place.
  • Reframed the product from "download a PDF report" to "a daily health companion" — Records section turns one-off lab values into HbA1c / Cholesterol / LDL trends over time.
  • Reports delivered in under 6 hours. Senior-citizen flat 10% off built into the home screen. Chat & Book Test as primary CTA.

The audience

Most diagnostic apps are designed for the wrong person — a young, urban, digitally fluent customer booking a one-off test. Torrent's actual patient base is different.

Asha · 64
Type 2 diabetic, 12 years
Tracks HbA1c every 3 months. Forgets last quarter's number by the time she sees the doctor. Freezes at empty search bars. Wants her last reading and an arrow showing better-or-worse.
Ravi · 41
Tracking father's chronic condition
Books his father's tests, reads the reports, decides whether to call the doctor. Wants the trend over 6 months at a glance. Either reassurance, or a clear "this needs a call."

The trap was designing for either Asha or Ravi. The product serves both — different contexts, different devices, sometimes for the same lab value.

The reframe

This isn't a booking app with reports attached. It's a daily companion for living with a chronic condition.

Once you frame it that way, every decision flips. The home screen surfaces your last reading, not "Book a test." Records are organised by value-over-time, not test-occasion. Interpretation is plain-language and quiet, not gamification with badges.

Accessibility deep-dive

"India's first accessibility-first diagnostic app" is worth standing behind only if accessibility is in the bones, not the QA pass.

  • 48pt minimum tap targets. Apple's HIG suggests 44; Asha's phone is rarely steady.
  • 16pt body minimum, 1.5 line-height. Captions are for timestamps, not content.
  • WCAG AA on every element, AAA on body. Tested in 5 lighting conditions including outdoor sun.
  • Voice-first paths for booking. Chat & Book Test is the primary CTA, not a power-user feature.
  • Regional language support. Per-language clinical review of Trend phrasings, not just translation.
  • No motion-as-information. Trend direction is shape + colour + text, not animation.

The hardest accessibility problem isn't checking boxes — it's not making the app look like a "senior app." Big tap targets that don't look big.

Trend-first design

Every other diagnostic app shows you a number. We show you a trend. The Trend Card has a strict reading order: plain-language interpretation first, value second, ideal range third, sparkline fourth.

Why interpretation first? In testing, when the value led, patients struggled to know whether 5.2 was good or bad. With interpretation first, the value becomes evidence — not a quiz.

The AI boundary

AI describes. AI does not prescribe.

AI generates plain-language interpretations of the patient's own historical values, OCRs paper prescriptions into suggested tests, routes free-text booking intent to the right package. AI does not provide medical advice, suggest treatment, or tell the patient what to do about a value.

The line sits between "your value is in/out of range" (statistical, app's job) and "you should do X about it" (medical, doctor's job). Phrasing templates are reviewed and approved by clinicians before any patient sees them.

Trade-offs

Senior-first means a calmer visual language than a younger-skewing app would have. Younger users adapt; senior users genuinely struggle with most consumer apps. Trends-first focus deferred doctor referral, e-pharmacy, and family-member account separation — adoption first, monetisation second.

The product is in the hands of patients now. The deeper measure of success is whether patients keep coming back to look at their trends — which is a 6-month signal we'll be watching carefully.

What I'd change

I'd have invested earlier in family member workflows. A surprising amount of trend-checking is done by adult children on behalf of parents. We deferred it to v2; if I'd front-loaded it, the early-adoption curve would likely have been steeper.

And: I'd have shipped doctor-shareable summary cards from day one. Patients want to bring their phone to a doctor visit and show "here's what's been changing." We have it on the roadmap; should have launched with it.
