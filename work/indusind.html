The economics of fewer decisions

A UX overhaul of high-stakes banking flows — loan applications, cross-currency transfers, account opening — reframed as a decision-reduction problem. Every removed decision is a recovered customer, a recovered application, a recovered revenue line.

RoleSenior Product Designer
TeamPwC India
TimelineJun 2024 — Nov 2024

In brief

  • The user problem wasn't "friction" — it was abandonment. Customers were dropping out of the highest-revenue flows (loan applications, cross-currency transfers) because the decision load was punitive. Each abandonment is a lost revenue line.
  • The bet: stop redesigning screens — redesign decisions. Every flow audited against one question: what's the minimum number of decisions to complete this?
  • How it landed: Apply for Business Loan compressed from 14 decisions to 6. Pending-action drift (the legacy bug class) dropped sharply. ~85% component reuse across the system.

The abandonment problem

The brief landed as "reduce friction in our customer flows." Friction is a feature word — it doesn't tell you which dollar is bleeding. The actual user problem is sharper, and so is the business consequence: customers were abandoning the bank's most valuable flows because the cognitive load of completing them was punitive. A business-loan applicant who drops out at decision 11 of 14 is not a UX statistic; they're a revenue line that walks to a competitor.

When we walked the 12 most-used flows in the legacy app and counted decisions, the picture was brutal:

  • Apply for Business Loan: 14 decisions across 9 screens. Half were re-asking for KYC the bank already had on file.
  • Cross-currency transfer: 11 decisions. Three were jargon ("correspondent bank", "charge bearer") the customer couldn't possibly know.
  • EMI prepayment: 8 decisions — three of them trick questions whose answer changed the next three.

Reframe: a "better screen" treats abandonment as a layout problem. The reality is each unnecessary decision is a measurable conversion drop. The flows we redesigned weren't the ugliest — they were the ones where the bank was losing the most revenue per drop-off.

Redesigning decisions

The strategic call: stop redesigning screens. Redesign decisions. The audit was uncomfortably simple — count every decision a customer had to make in a flow, then ask: does the bank already have the answer? If yes, that's not a decision. That's a confirmation.

What we explicitly chose not to optimize for: visual polish on the legacy structure. A screen-by-screen polish would have been faster, demoed easily, and changed nothing about the abandonment economics. We held back from "redesign everything" in favour of "remove what shouldn't have been there."

Killed: a wizard mode that walked customers through every complex flow. Tested with users — patronising on simple steps, confusing on complex ones. Replaced with progressive disclosure inside a single continuous frame.

The seven decisions that disappeared weren't simplified — they were moved off the customer's plate entirely. Replaced with confirmation chips: "Use this PAN" / "Use this address." Customer taps once instead of typing.

"Needs your attention"

A single home-dashboard primitive surfacing exactly what blocks customer progress — pending approvals, incomplete applications, EMIs due. Three rules:

  • Action-led, not status-led. Not "Loan application 50% complete." Instead: "Continue your business loan — 4 fields left."
  • One-tap to resolve. The chip lands the customer on the field that needs them, not on the start of the form.
  • Quiet when there's nothing. No "All caught up!" trophy. The empty state is silence.

Pre-fill + smart defaults

Three patterns across every flow: confirmation chips instead of blank input fields; defaults from history (the beneficiary you transferred to 8 of the last 10 times); live computation as the customer types (loan EMI updates inline, no "Calculate" button).

Killed a wizard mode that walked customers through every complex flow. Tested with users — patronising on simple steps, confusing on complex ones. Replaced with progressive disclosure inside one continuous frame.

System foundation

INDIE shipped on a unified design system — semantic tokens, ~120 components, mobile + web from one library. ~85% component reuse across surfaces. The AI Figma plugins I now use everywhere (Validator, CLAUDE.md Exporter, Generator) were all born here.

Business outcome

Decision-reduction tied to outcomes the bank could see on its own dashboards:

↑ Completion
High-stakes flows (business loan, cross-currency transfer) compressed to fewer decisions — directly recovering applicants who would have dropped at decision 11+.
↓ Pending-action drift
The legacy bug class — customers stuck mid-flow with no clear path forward — collapsed sharply once "Needs your attention" replaced status-led messaging.
~85% reuse
Component reuse across mobile + web. Same tokens, same components — meaning the next flow gets cheaper, not more expensive.

The clearest outcome isn't on the metric tiles: the system survives without me. Designers who've never met me are shipping inside it. That's the partner-level bar — leverage that compounds after I'm gone.

What we didn't ship

Three calls we made deliberately, and would still make again — but worth being honest about the cost:

  • Pre-fill assumes clean data. The first month had a small spike in support tickets when stale KYC surfaced incorrectly. Volume dropped once data quality improved, but the experience for those early customers was worse than the legacy app — and the data quality work should have been a v0 dependency, not a v1.5 fix.
  • The contribution tooling. PR templates, automated changelogs, contribution rituals — we shipped them in month four. Done in month one, the contribution velocity from other designers would have been double across the engagement. A real cost paid in the team's ramp time.
  • The governance doc. Six bullet points on what counts as a system decision vs a vertical decision. Written in month three, not month one. Cost us roughly two weeks of consolidation work that should have been free. Writing it earlier would have been the cheapest investment in the whole project.
