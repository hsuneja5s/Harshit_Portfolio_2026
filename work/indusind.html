TL;DR

  • Compressed high-friction banking flows (account opening, business loan applications, cross-currency transfers) into few-step, error-proof journeys by collapsing decisions and pre-filling state.
  • Built and rolled out a unified design system underneath — same tokens, same components — across customer mobile, web, and internal staff tools.
  • Component reuse hit ~85% across surfaces. Pending-action drift, the most common bug class on the legacy app, dropped sharply.

The constraint

IndusInd's customer experience had years of organic feature growth. Mobile and web had drifted apart. The same "transfer money" flow looked, behaved, and was named three different ways across surfaces. Customers noticed. So did regulators, who flagged inconsistency in disclosures.

More urgent than the cosmetic issue: the high-friction flows. Applying for a business loan was a 14-step process across multiple disconnected pages. Cross-currency transfers required customers to choose options they couldn't possibly understand without a banker on the phone. EMI handling and pending approvals piled up because customers didn't know what they needed to do next.

The mandate: shorten the journey, surface what needs attention, prevent errors before they happen. Without freezing the product to do it.

The decision

The core call: stop redesigning screens, start redesigning journeys. Every flow audit started with the same question — "what's the minimum number of decisions a customer should make to complete this?" — and worked backward from there.

Chose
A "Needs your attention" strip on the home dashboard — pending approvals, incomplete applications, EMIs due — surfacing exactly what blocks progress, with one-tap entry to resolve.
Chose
"At your fingertips" customisable shortcuts on home — Share QR, Scan & Pay, Bill Pay, Apply for Business Loan — letting customers pin their own most-used actions.
Chose
Smart pre-fill from KYC + transaction history. Most form fields a customer would have to type are pre-populated and shown as confirmation chips, not blank inputs.
Chose
Semantic tokens — not literal — even though semantic cost more upfront. Banking has long surface-level requirements (dark mode, regional themes, white-label for partner products) that literal tokens couldn't have absorbed.
Killed
A "wizard mode" with separate step-by-step screens for every complex flow. Tested with target users — felt patronising on the simple steps, confusing on the complex ones. Replaced with progressive disclosure inside one continuous frame.
Killed
A separate "Pro" surface for power users. Maintaining two surfaces is governance debt we weren't going to pay. Power-user affordances (saved beneficiaries, recent recipients, gesture shortcuts) are layered on top of the same surface.

The system

The redesign is anchored on a journey-first component library. Each high-volume task type — transfer, pay bill, deposit, investment, loan application, dispute — gets one canonical flow composition. Engineers ship new tasks by extending the registry, not by designing new screens from scratch.

Three layers feed the system: tokens (Figma Variables via Tokens Studio) sync to Style Dictionary in CI/CD, which generates platform-specific outputs (CSS variables for web, XML for Android, JSON for React Native). Designers change a token in Figma → engineering picks up the diff in a PR.

The governance doc is short on purpose: "any change that affects more than one team's UI is a system change." That sentence has prevented more drift than any tooling we built.

AI in the loop

This is the project where I started building tools to handle scale. Three concrete uses:

  • Design System Validator: caught hundreds of token-mismatch and structural breaks before they landed in PR. Built because manual review across 6 consumer designers wasn't scaling.
  • CLAUDE.md Exporter: when engineers wanted to scaffold prototypes via Claude Code, they kept generating components that didn't match our tokens. The exporter packages our system as a single CLAUDE.md so Claude Code builds inside the system by default.
  • Design System Generator: we forked it for the IndusInd internal-tools team — instead of waiting weeks for system support, they could spin up a token-aligned starter system for a new internal app in a day.

Every plugin started as a "I'm doing this manually 30 times a week" moment. None were built as side projects in isolation; all were built for this team and the work itself.

Trade-offs

Choosing semantic tokens cost us a quarter of upfront design + engineering work compared to literal. We absorbed it. Two years from now, when dark mode lands in a week instead of a quarter, that bill will pay itself back many times over.

Concurrent migration meant some features shipped during the transition were temporarily inconsistent — a "what's on the system" sticker on every Figma frame helped engineers know what they could trust. Imperfect, but better than freezing product velocity for six months.

Killing the wizard-mode meant some genuinely complex flows now ask customers to think a little more in a single step. We accepted it. The path-of-least-thinking is fewer steps with smart defaults — not more steps with hand-holding.

What I'd change

I'd have written the governance doc on day one, not month three. We had drift across the team's outputs until we sat down and articulated "what counts as a system decision vs. a vertical decision." Cost us ~2 weeks of consolidation. The doc itself is six bullet points; writing it earlier would have been free.

And: I'd have invested in contribution tooling (PR templates, automated changelogs, contribution rituals) sooner. We did them in month four. Done at month one, the contribution velocity would have been double.
