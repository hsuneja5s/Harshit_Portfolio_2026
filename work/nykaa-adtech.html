From approval queues to self-serve campaign management

Nykaa's ad business hit a revenue ceiling because every campaign needed a Nykaa rep in the loop. The product wasn't a self-serve console — it was the operational unbundling of an account-management bottleneck. Adoption across thousands of brand managers, support tickets down sharply, AdOps freed for strategic work.

RoleSenior Product Designer
TeamNykaa
Timeline2022 — 2023

In brief

  • The user problem wasn't "too many emails" — it was a revenue ceiling. Every campaign required a Nykaa rep in the loop, capping how many brands the platform could serve. Self-serve was the only way the line item could grow.
  • The bet: design for Priya (a brand manager doing 20% of her week on Nykaa), not for an ad-ops specialist. A 3-tab IA, refused fourth tab, status visibility everywhere. Not a Google-Ads lookalike.
  • How it landed: adoption across thousands of brand-manager seats. Support ticket volume dropped sharply. AdOps freed for strategic conversations they were never able to have during the concierge era.

A revenue-ceiling problem

The brief landed as "give brand managers a self-serve ads console." Reading it that way produces a faster version of the existing concierge model — a faster intake form. The actual user problem was upstream and harsher: Nykaa's ad-revenue line was capped by the throughput of its own operations team.

The legacy flow: brand manager emails Nykaa rep → rep configures campaign → rep emails screenshot for approval → campaign goes live 3-7 days later → PDF report 2 weeks in. Scaled to 50 brands. Broke at 500. Was on fire at thousands. Every brand added a fixed amount of human-hours to AdOps — and AdOps doesn't scale linearly with the inventory Nykaa has to monetise. The result: a structural gap between the inventory available and the revenue the ad business could capture against it.

So the design problem isn't "give the brand manager a nicer interface." It's "unbundle the concierge so the platform can monetise its full inventory." Two different products live underneath those two framings.

Build the workflow Priya already has in her head. Remove every step that doesn't add value to her — and that doesn't need to live inside Nykaa's AdOps team.

The bet

The non-obvious calls — the ones that look like restraint and read like missed-features in a demo:

  • Designed for Priya, not for ad-ops specialists. Most ad platforms (Google Ads, Meta Ads Manager) are built for daily users who learn the surface. Brand managers spend 20% of their week on Nykaa. Power-user range was the wrong target — comprehension on first open was.
  • Three tabs. Refused a fourth. Three is the cognitive ceiling for a non-daily user. A fourth tab would have absorbed real wishlist items (multi-brand reporting, advanced targeting, drag-drop builder). We made the call to ship none of those in v1 — adoption depended on legibility, not capability.

What we explicitly chose not to optimize for: feature parity with Google Ads. We could have built a sophisticated targeting console; brand managers would have ignored it. Restraint was the strategy.

Designing for Priya

The person we designed for is the test of whether the bet was right. Not a hypothetical — a composite from 14 brand-manager interviews:

Priya · Brand Manager, HUL
Owns 3 brands · ~12 campaigns / quarter
Plans seasonal campaigns. Briefs creative agencies. Tracks against brand OKRs. Spends 20% of her week on Nykaa, 80% across other channels.
Not a daily ad-platform user. Not a designer. Wants creative live, performance visible, status clear. Wants performance she can paste into a Monday slide.

Composite persona built from 14 brand-manager interviews across beauty, FMCG, and fashion.

3-tab IA

Three jobs brand managers actually do. Each its own tab. We refused to add a fourth.

Ads
Launch a campaign. Upload a creative. See its status.
Sections
Performance breakdown by placement — Banner, Category in Focus, In the Spotlight.
Tags
Lightweight audience + targeting layer. Not the Google-Ads-style power-user surface.

Three is the cognitive ceiling for a non-daily user. Killed in v1: drag-drop campaign builder, advanced targeting, multi-brand reporting. All right calls eventually — not in v1.

Creative review states

Most-asked question in testing: "What's happening with my ad?" So the answer is visible at all times, on every creative.

Pending Approval
In Nykaa review queue. Status visible.
→
Change Required
Specific feedback. Action next to the issue.
→
Approved · Live
Live on Nykaa. Metrics flow in real-time.

State is rendered per creative format, not per campaign. A campaign might have Square approved, Horizontal pending, Vertical needing changes. Brand managers see exactly where to focus.

Performance hierarchy

Six metrics, in funnel order: Impressions, Clicks, CTR, Add to Cart, Orders, Revenue. Same metrics at every scope — campaign, ad, section, tag. Reading right-to-left answers the only question that matters: is the spend paying off?

Above the strip: Attribution Window as a primary chip (1 Day / 14 Days). Most platforms bury this in advanced settings. The answer to "is this paying off?" is meaningless without it.

Business outcome

The shift to self-serve tied directly to outcomes Nykaa's ad business was structurally capped on:

↑ Inventory monetisation
The revenue ceiling lifts when AdOps stops being the bottleneck. Adoption across thousands of brand-manager seats — each one a campaign that previously needed human-hours to launch.
↓ Support load
Sharp reduction in support tickets from brand managers. Status visibility on every creative collapses the "what's happening with my ad?" volume that was eating AdOps' day.
↑ Strategic AdOps
AdOps freed for the conversations they were never able to have under concierge — campaign planning, creative direction, partnership strategy. The team's value to the business stepped up, not down.

Faster time-to-market for every campaign was the obvious win. The bigger one was structural: Nykaa's ad business stopped being a function of its operations team's headcount.

What we didn't ship

Three things we wanted in v1 and made the call to defer. Worth being explicit about each:

  • Onboarding for first-time brand managers. The platform was obvious from cold to most users — but a 2-minute guided first-campaign flow would have raised week-1 retention meaningfully. Added in v2; should have been v1. A real cost paid in slower activation among the most engaged brand-manager segment.
  • Cross-campaign portfolio dashboard. Brand managers running 5+ campaigns at once needed a "portfolio view" that we didn't ship in v1. They got it in v2 — but we left some of the most engaged users frustrated for a quarter. Holding the 3-tab line was the right call; not having a portfolio surface for power-users was the wrong cost to pay.
  • Multi-brand reporting and advanced targeting. Deferred deliberately, and we'd defer them again. But it's worth being honest — the v1 platform left a small group of sophisticated users without the surface they needed. They came back when v2 shipped, but a few brand-manager-of-the-year-types absorbed real friction in the meantime.
