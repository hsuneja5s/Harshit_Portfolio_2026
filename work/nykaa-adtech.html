From approval queues to self-serve campaign management

Giving brand managers a self-serve ads console. A single place to launch placements on Nykaa, see live performance, and iterate creatives — reducing manual coordination and speeding up time-to-market for new campaigns.

In brief

  • Built a self-serve campaign console from scratch — brand managers launch placements on Nykaa without a Nykaa rep in the loop.
  • Designed a unified live performance view (Impressions, Clicks, CTR, Add to Cart, Orders, Revenue) with section-level breakdowns across Banner, Category in Focus, In the Spotlight.
  • Drove adoption across thousands of brand-manager seats and reduced support tickets significantly. Faster go-to-market for every campaign on Nykaa.

Brand-manager persona

Most ad platforms (Google Ads, Meta Ads Manager) are built for ad-ops specialists who use them daily. Nykaa's brand managers are not those people.

Priya · Brand Manager, HUL
Owns 3 brands · ~12 campaigns / quarter
Plans seasonal campaigns. Briefs creative agencies. Tracks against brand OKRs. Spends 20% of her week on Nykaa, 80% across other channels.
Not a daily ad-platform user. Not a designer. Wants creative live, performance visible, status clear. Wants performance she can paste into a Monday slide.

Composite persona built from 14 brand-manager interviews across beauty, FMCG, and fashion.

From concierge to self-serve

Before this platform: brand manager emails Nykaa rep → rep configures campaign → rep emails screenshot for approval → campaign goes live 3-7 days later → PDF report 2 weeks in. Scaled to 50 brands. Broke at 500. On fire at thousands.

Build the workflow Priya already has in her head. Remove every step that doesn't add value to her.

3-tab IA

Three jobs brand managers actually do. Each its own tab. We refused to add a fourth.

Ads
Launch a campaign. Upload a creative. See its status.
Sections
Performance breakdown by placement — Banner, Category in Focus, In the Spotlight.
Tags
Lightweight audience + targeting layer. Not the Google-Ads-style power-user surface.

Three is the cognitive ceiling for a non-daily user. Killed in v1: drag-drop campaign builder, advanced targeting, multi-brand reporting. All right calls eventually — not in v1.

Creative review states

Most-asked question in testing: "What's happening with my ad?" So the answer is visible at all times, on every creative.

Pending Approval
In Nykaa review queue. Status visible.
→
Change Required
Specific feedback. Action next to the issue.
→
Approved · Live
Live on Nykaa. Metrics flow in real-time.

State is rendered per creative format, not per campaign. A campaign might have Square approved, Horizontal pending, Vertical needing changes. Brand managers see exactly where to focus.

Performance hierarchy

Six metrics, in funnel order: Impressions, Clicks, CTR, Add to Cart, Orders, Revenue. Same metrics at every scope — campaign, ad, section, tag. Reading right-to-left answers the only question that matters: is the spend paying off?

Above the strip: Attribution Window as a primary chip (1 Day / 14 Days). Most platforms bury this in advanced settings. The answer to "is this paying off?" is meaningless without it.

Trade-offs

Three-tabs-only lost a small group of power users doing more sophisticated work (multi-brand reporting, custom segments). They came back in v2. Auto-fitting creatives sometimes made aesthetic calls a brand manager would have made differently — "Change Required" was the safety valve.

3

The platform freed up Nykaa's ad-ops team for genuinely strategic conversations — campaign planning, creative direction, partnership strategy — instead of campaign config. Brand managers got the speed they needed to test more ideas in less time.

What I'd change

I'd have invested in onboarding for first-time brand managers sooner. Our launch focused on making the platform obvious from cold, and it mostly was — but a 2-minute guided first-campaign flow would have raised week-1 retention. We added it in v2; should have been v1.

And: I'd have shipped a cross-campaign dashboard from day one. Brand managers running 5+ campaigns at once needed a "portfolio view" that we didn't ship in v1. They got it in v2 — but we left some of the most engaged users frustrated for a quarter.
