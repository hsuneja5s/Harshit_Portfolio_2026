Nykaa — AdTech Platform

Giving brand managers a self-serve ads console. A single place to launch placements on Nykaa, see live performance, and iterate creatives — reducing manual coordination and speeding up time-to-market for new campaigns.

RoleProduct Designer · End-to-end product design
CompanyNykaa (FSN E-Commerce Ventures)
UsersBrand managers at Nykaa partner brands (HUL, L'Oréal, Mamaearth, hundreds more)
DomainB2B SaaS · AdTech · E-commerce platform
StackFigma, Nykaa internal design system, internal ad-serving APIs

TL;DR

  • Built a self-serve campaign console from scratch — brand managers launch placements on Nykaa without a Nykaa rep in the loop.
  • Designed a unified live performance view (Impressions, Clicks, CTR, Add to Cart, Orders, Revenue) with section-level breakdowns across Banner, Category in Focus, In the Spotlight.
  • Drove adoption across thousands of brand-manager seats and reduced support tickets significantly. Faster go-to-market for every campaign on Nykaa.

The constraint

Nykaa's ad platform was historically run as a concierge service. Brand managers emailed Nykaa reps with creative briefs, briefs went into spreadsheets, reps configured campaigns manually, performance reports came back as PDFs days later. As Nykaa scaled to thousands of partner brands, this became unsustainable — for the brands and for Nykaa.

The mandate: collapse the loop. Brand managers should be able to log in, launch a campaign, see it go live, and watch performance update — without ever talking to anyone at Nykaa. Reps should be freed up for strategic conversations, not campaign config.

Hard constraint: brand managers are not designers, marketers, or ad-platform power users. They're product owners who happen to own a marketing budget. The platform had to be approachable from cold — no training program, no onboarding gate.

The decision

The product call: three workflows, one nav. Most ad platforms (Google Ads, Meta Ads Manager) offer infinite configurability and overwhelm anyone who isn't a daily user. Nykaa's platform should do the opposite — show brand managers exactly the three things they actually do.

Chose
Three primary tabs only: Ads (campaign launch + creative), Sections (placement performance), Tags (audience + targeting). Everything in the platform fits one of these three jobs.
Chose
A unified metric strip — Impressions, Clicks, CTR, Add to Cart, # Orders, Revenue — visible at every level (campaign, ad, section, tag). Same metrics, same vocabulary, everywhere.
Chose
Attribution Window controls (1 Day / 14 Days) surfaced as primary chips, not buried in advanced settings. Every report in the platform respects the brand manager's chosen window.
Chose
A creative-review workflow with explicit states (Pending Creative Approval, Change Required, Approved). The brand manager never wonders "what's the status of my ad" — it's stamped on the ad.
Killed
A drag-drop campaign builder. Tested with brand managers — they didn't want to design layouts, they wanted to upload one creative and have it auto-fit Nykaa's placement formats (Square 1:1, Horizontal 2:1, Vertical 3:4, Horizontal 4:1).
Killed
A "negative keywords" / advanced targeting feature in v1. Right call long-term, but most brand managers in v1 didn't even use the basic targeting — adding power-user features early would have buried what mattered.

The system

The platform is composed from a small set of data-shaped components — metric tiles, performance charts, attribution chips, section breakdowns, creative-state badges. Every screen in the platform is some combination of these primitives applied to a specific scope (campaign, ad, section, tag).

The Request Ad flow is a single-page form composed from three blocks: Ad Request Detail (name, brand, daily budget, dates, ad format), Campaign Overview (status, start/end, brand group, rate card), Creative (primary text, secondary text, category, destination URL, image upload across 3 formats with live preview).

The image upload component is the most-tested part of the system. Brand managers upload one creative; the platform fits it across Nykaa's 4 placement formats and surfaces "Change Required" on any format that needs adjustment. Asymmetric formats are the long-tail bug the rest of the industry's ad platforms ignore.

AI in the loop

AI on this product is intentionally minimal — brand managers are spending real money, and they need predictability over surprise. Where AI helps:

  • Creative-fit suggestions: when a brand manager uploads a single image, the platform auto-suggests crops for the 4 placement formats. Brand manager confirms — AI suggests, brand manager decides.
  • Performance anomaly flags: a quiet alert surfaces if a campaign's CTR or revenue drops sharply. No autoplay-style "AI insights" — just a flag the manager can act on.
  • Microcopy review: all platform copy passed through Claude as a tone-and-clarity check during the build. Caught dozens of jargon-y phrasings before they shipped to brand managers.

Where AI doesn't: budget recommendations, audience targeting suggestions, automatic bidding. Brand managers are accountable to their own brands — taking that accountability away (even with helpful AI) breaks trust.

Trade-offs

Three-tabs-only meant some advanced workflows (multi-brand reporting, custom segment builders) didn't make v1. We lost a small group of power users who were doing more sophisticated work. They came back in v2 — but the cost was real.

Auto-fitting creatives across 4 formats meant the platform sometimes made aesthetic calls a brand manager would have made differently. The "Change Required" state was the safety valve, but it added review cycles. Net positive — brand managers ship more campaigns, faster — but it wasn't free.

Restricting to Nykaa's 4 native placement formats (Banner, Category in Focus, In the Spotlight, others) limited integration with off-Nykaa ad platforms. Right call for a self-serve console — but the next step is a unified view of on- and off-Nykaa performance.

What I'd change

I'd have invested in onboarding for first-time brand managers sooner. Our launch focused on making the platform obvious from cold, and it mostly was — but a 2-minute guided first-campaign flow would have raised week-1 retention. We added it in v2; should have been v1.

And: I'd have shipped a cross-campaign dashboard from day one. Brand managers running 5+ campaigns at once needed a "portfolio view" that we didn't ship in v1. They got it in v2 — but we left some of the most engaged users frustrated for a quarter.
