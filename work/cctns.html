The federation problem

India is not one design problem. It's the same problem multiplied by every variance axis — language, literacy, device, trust, state machinery, regulation. Designing one product for the whole country means designing for the variance, not around it.

Languages
Hindi, English + 4 regional at launch. Each is culturally-aware tone, not translation.
Literacy
From metro lawyer on iPhone to rural farmer who has never typed on a phone. Voice-first is the primary path.
Device + bandwidth
iPhone on 5G to ₹3,000 Android on 2G. Every page has an offline-first fallback.
State machinery
28 states + 8 UTs, each with its own legacy systems and political ownership. UI decisions can be vetoed at the state level.
Regulatory shell
MHA security review · RPwD Act · data localisation · evidence-handling rules. Every screen clears multiple gates.

Unified-with-flexibility

The strategic question wasn't "what should the citizen experience look like?" It was "should there be one citizen experience, or 28?"

Easy path: give each state a customisable surface — re-skin, re-arrange, re-name. That replicates the fragmentation we were trying to fix. The harder call, at real political cost, was one citizen surface across India, with state-specific logic in the backend. One vocabulary; one mental model a citizen carries from Maharashtra to Karnataka without re-learning.

Flexibility lives in customisation slots, not the surface. States can adapt language, local terminology, minor flow steps. They cannot fork the citizen surface, the navigation, or the data shape.

Leadership & governance

5 designers across 5 citizen verticals shipping in parallel. No designer can hold the whole system in their head. Governance is the infrastructure, not a phase.

  • One designer, one vertical, end-to-end. Ownership before scale. Cross-vertical decisions come back to me; depth stays with them.
  • Weekly system reviews. Every Friday, 90 minutes. Each designer presents one decision. The team learns each other's domains.
  • "Affects more than one team's UI" = system change. One sentence — most-cited governance principle on the project.
  • 1-page RFC for new patterns. 48-hour review window. Speed without bottleneck.

The senior move: trusting depth. The alternative is becoming the bottleneck for 5 verticals of micro-decisions.

AI in regulated work

AI accelerates design work, not officer work. AI lives upstream — in how the team builds.

  • Multilingual flow generation via Conversation Flow Generator. Per-language draft cost dropped from weeks to hours; native-speaker review still required.
  • System validation via Design System Validator — manual token review didn't scale across 5 designers.
  • Code prototyping for kiosk + voice flows via CLAUDE.md Exporter — Claude Code generates inside our system tokens.

AI does not appear in any flow involving evidence, complainant identity, or officer judgement. The boundary is a contract — speed in tooling, deterministic UX in the product.

Trade-offs

Unified UI cost political capital with state IT departments. Voice-first flows are slower for power users than dense forms. Real-time citizen-officer chat was killed by compliance review and replaced with structured request/response flows. In compliance-heavy work, the question isn't "can we ship this?" — it's "can the regulator look at this in 6 months?"

What I'd change

I would have invested earlier in field research with non-metro officers. We did the citizen-side ethnography well, but our officer assumptions were Delhi-coded for the first quarter, and we burned cycles redesigning officer flows once we got to ground truth in the field. The lesson stuck: in regulated multi-stakeholder work, every type of user needs first-hand contact — not just the headline one.

Also: I'd have written the governance model on day one, not month three. We had drift across the team's outputs until we sat down and articulated "what counts as a system decision vs. a vertical decision." Cost us two weeks of consolidation that should have been free.
