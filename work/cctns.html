TL;DR

  • Leading design on a national policing platform spanning citizen services, officer dashboards, and FIR management — designed for India's full citizen base.
  • Reframed a fragmented multi-state experience into one citizen surface with state-specific logic in the backend — abstracted variance without papering over it.
  • Established design system, governance model, and AI-assisted workflows for the team. Mentoring 5 designers across the engagement.

The constraint

Designing for India's full citizen base means designing for extreme variance at every axis — language, literacy, device, bandwidth, trust. A user could be a metro lawyer filing online from an iPhone, or a rural farmer dictating a complaint over a 2G connection at a village outpost.

Layered on top: a federation of state police forces, each with their own legacy systems, terminology, and political ownership. Any UI we built had to respect state-specific workflows. And being a Ministry of Home Affairs project, every screen had to clear security review, accessibility (RPwD Act), and data-localisation review.

Six languages at launch. Offline-first by default. Citizens whose first contact with government services is this app. The bar for clarity was not "can a designer use this" — it was "can my grandmother in a village use this."

The decision

The core call was whether to build one citizen experience that abstracts state differences, or a federated experience where each state customises. We picked the first — at significant political cost — because anything else would replicate the existing fragmentation that made the problem worth solving in the first place.

Chose
A unified citizen surface with state-specific logic in the backend. One UI vocabulary across India.
Killed
Per-state UI templating. Dropped because it would have calcified state silos forever.
Chose
Voice-first citizen flows for low-literacy users — designed as the primary path, not framed as an "accessibility feature."
Chose
A mentorship model where each junior designer owned one citizen vertical end-to-end. Ownership before scale.
Killed
Real-time chat between citizens and officers on the platform. Compliance review couldn't approve unfiltered messaging on a national platform — replaced with structured request/response flows.

The system

The platform's backbone is a three-tier design system — semantic tokens at the core, vertical-specific component variants in the middle, state-customisation slots at the surface. This lets us ship one citizen app while letting states adapt language, terminology, and minor flow steps without forking the codebase.

Component scope at launch covers the full citizen + officer flow surface, all wired through Tokens Studio with an automated pipeline into the engineering team's Storybook.

AI in the loop

This project is where I started leaning hard into AI-assisted design workflows. Three concrete uses:

  • Multilingual flow generation: drafting voice-first flows in 6 languages would have taken weeks of per-language work. I built (and now use across PwC engagements) a Figma plugin — Conversation Flow Generator — that uses Anthropic's models to generate localised flow variants from a single English source.
  • System validation at scale: with the component count, language matrix, and vertical count we had, manual review of token usage didn't scale. The Design System Validator plugin I built flags structural breaks before engineering handoff.
  • Claude Code for prototyping: citizen flows that needed real-time interaction (kiosk mode, voice playback) were prototyped in code, not Figma. The CLAUDE.md Exporter plugin lets Claude Code build those prototypes inside our system tokens automatically.

Where I didn't use AI: any flow involving evidence, complainant identity, or officer judgement. Designing those with AI in the loop would have been irresponsible at this scale, and would have failed compliance review on first pass.

Trade-offs

The unified-UI decision cost us political capital with state IT departments who wanted their own branding on the citizen surface. We absorbed that — escalated, lost a battle, kept the larger war. The system survives.

Voice-first flows are slower for power users (lawyers, NGOs filing repeatedly) than a dense form. We accepted that. The platform's ground-level legitimacy depends on the rural farmer being able to use it, not the lawyer being optimised for.

What I'd change

I would have invested earlier in field research with non-metro officers. We did the citizen-side ethnography well, but our officer assumptions were Delhi-coded for the first quarter, and we burned cycles redesigning officer flows once we got to ground truth in the field. The lesson stuck: in regulated multi-stakeholder work, every type of user needs first-hand contact — not just the headline one.

Also: I'd have written the governance model on day one, not month three. We had drift across the team's outputs until we sat down and articulated "what counts as a system decision vs. a vertical decision." Cost us two weeks of consolidation that should have been free.
