Designing citizen trust into a national platform

A national policing platform connecting citizens, officers, and state machinery across India. The core question wasn't "what should the UI look like" — it was whether 1.4B citizens would trust a digital surface enough to use it instead of walking into a station.

In brief

  • The real problem wasn't UX — it was institutional trust. A platform that looks great but a citizen doesn't trust enough to use against the local station has zero impact.
  • The strategic call: one citizen surface across India (with state-specific logic in the backend), not 28 customised state surfaces. Political cost upfront, federation-proof at scale.
  • How it gets paid back: citizen adoption against the legacy "walk into the station" path — the only metric that matters for state digitisation budgets to keep flowing.

The trust problem

The unstated assumption in most government-tech briefs is: "if we build it, citizens will use it." That's wrong, and it's the most expensive assumption in the category. A policing platform competes against a citizen's default — walking into a station and talking to a person they at least know how to read. To get used at scale, the digital surface has to clear a higher trust bar than the physical one.

Trust at this scale is not a microcopy problem. It's a function of: legibility (can the citizen read the screen?), legitimacy (does it look and behave like the state, not like a startup?), reachability (does it work on a ₹3,000 phone in a regional language?), and recoverability (when something goes wrong, is there a human?). Miss any one of those and adoption stalls — which means state IT budgets shift back to physical infrastructure, and the platform quietly dies.

Treating this as a "redesign citizen services" brief would have produced a beautiful, unused product. The reframe was: every design decision is a trust deposit or a trust withdrawal.

The strategic call

The pivotal question wasn't "what should the citizen experience look like?" It was "should there be one citizen experience, or 28?"

The easy path was give each state a customisable surface — re-skin, re-arrange, re-name. That replicates the fragmentation we were trying to fix. The harder call, at real political cost with state IT departments, was one citizen surface across India, with state-specific logic in the backend. One vocabulary; one mental model a citizen carries from Maharashtra to Karnataka without re-learning.

What we explicitly chose not to optimize for: state-level customisation surface area. Flexibility lives in customisation slots — language, local terminology, minor flow steps. States cannot fork the citizen surface, the navigation, or the data shape. We absorbed political friction in exchange for a federation-proof product. Partner-level call.

Federation map

One product. Every variance axis. Designing for the variance, not around it:

Languages
Hindi, English + 4 regional at launch. Each is culturally-aware tone, not translation.
Literacy
From metro lawyer on iPhone to rural farmer who has never typed on a phone. Voice-first is the primary path.
Device + bandwidth
iPhone on 5G to ₹3,000 Android on 2G. Every page has an offline-first fallback.
State machinery
28 states + 8 UTs, each with its own legacy systems and political ownership. UI decisions can be vetoed at the state level.
Regulatory shell
MHA security review · RPwD Act · data localisation · evidence-handling rules. Every screen clears multiple gates.

Shipping at scale

5 designers across 5 citizen verticals shipping in parallel. No designer can hold the whole system in their head. Governance is the infrastructure, not a phase.

  • One designer, one vertical, end-to-end. Ownership before scale. Cross-vertical decisions come back to me; depth stays with them.
  • Weekly system reviews. Every Friday, 90 minutes. Each designer presents one decision. The team learns each other's domains.
  • "Affects more than one team's UI" = system change. One sentence — most-cited governance principle on the project.
  • 1-page RFC for new patterns. 48-hour review window. Speed without bottleneck.

The senior move: trusting depth. The alternative is becoming the bottleneck for 5 verticals of micro-decisions.

AI in regulated work

AI accelerates design work, not officer work. AI lives upstream — in how the team builds.

  • Multilingual flow generation via Conversation Flow Generator. Per-language draft cost dropped from weeks to hours; native-speaker review still required.
  • System validation via Design System Validator — manual token review didn't scale across 5 designers.
  • Code prototyping for kiosk + voice flows via CLAUDE.md Exporter — Claude Code generates inside our system tokens.

AI does not appear in any flow involving evidence, complainant identity, or officer judgement. The boundary is a contract — speed in tooling, deterministic UX in the product.

Where it earned its keep

The design decisions tied directly to outcomes that matter for a national platform's continued funding — not vanity metrics:

↑ Citizen adoption
Reach across India's full citizen base — the only metric that earns the next phase of state IT budget. Pilot numbers under NDA.
↓ Time-to-resolution
Citizens resolving requests through the digital surface instead of the station queue. Direct relief on officer workload.
28 + 8
States + UTs on a single citizen surface — federation-proof, no forking. The strategic call paid back.

State-level adoption rates, officer satisfaction scores, and citizen-side trust metrics are part of the deeper case study available under NDA.

What we didn't ship

Three things were on the list and stayed on the list. Worth being honest:

  • Real-time citizen-officer chat. Killed by compliance review — chat creates evidentiary records the legal framework wasn't ready for. Replaced with structured request/response flows. The right call for v1, but it costs us the warm-touch interaction citizens want when they're escalating a real complaint.
  • State-customisable citizen surface. Several state IT departments wanted skin-level customisation. We held the line on a single surface, knowing it cost us political capital. The right call long-term, but it means we win each state the hard way — through citizen adoption pulling state ownership in, not the reverse.
  • Voice-first power-user paths. Voice is the primary flow for low-literacy citizens, which slows down the metro lawyer who'd rather just fill a dense form. We accepted that asymmetry. A "power-user mode" would have been a v1 distraction — but it's an honest debt the v2 needs to address.
